TITLE: MIT License as the project's software license

DATE: 2026-04-09

STATUS: accepted

AUTHOR: Andrii Chemerysov


CONTEXT

The project is open source and requires a license that governs how the code,
documentation, and research artifacts may be used, modified, and redistributed.
The license choice signals the project's values, determines contributor
expectations, and affects how the project's work propagates through the
ecosystem.

DECISION

The project is licensed under the MIT License. The copyright line reads:
Copyright (c) 2026 Andrii Chemerysov and contributors. As the project grows, a
transition to a foundation or similar structure holding the copyright
collectively remains a possibility and would be recorded as a separate decision.

ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED

GPL v3: Requires derivative works to also be released under GPL, making openness
self-propagating. Appealing in principle because it prevents the project's work
from being incorporated into closed proprietary products. Rejected because
enforcement of any license depends on the capacity to litigate, which the
project does not currently have, making the reciprocity guarantee largely
theoretical. The friction GPL introduces for contributors and downstream users
is concrete while the protection it offers is not.

AGPL v3: Extends GPL's reciprocity to cover software run as a service, closing
the network loophole that would otherwise allow a proprietary service to run the
project's code without releasing modifications. The most protective option
against the specific risk of a finance company taking the project's research and
running it as a closed product. Rejected for the same enforcement reasons as
GPL, and additionally because AGPL is the most contributor-friction-heavy of the
common licenses, with some organisations having blanket policies against using
AGPL software.

CC BY-SA for everything: Creative Commons ShareAlike applied uniformly to all
project content, treating the project as a knowledge artifact rather than a
software product. Appealing because it refuses the distinction between code and
research that the project itself is trying to dissolve. Rejected because CC
licenses were not designed for software, are silent on patent rights, and
produce legal ambiguity around what constitutes a derivative work of code.
Contributor friction is also significant, as many contributors operate under
employer policies that cover recognised software licenses but may not address CC
licensing.

CC BY-SA for documentation and research, MIT for code: A dual license structure
mapping onto the two kinds of artifact the project contains. Appealing for the
same reasons as CC BY-SA alone and less legally ambiguous than applying CC to
code. Rejected because the boundary between code and research in this project is
intentionally blurred, the model is the paper, the interaction is the argument,
and maintaining a dual license requires that boundary to be clear, policed, and
adjudicated whenever something sits on the line. The administrative overhead is
not justified by a protection that remains difficult to enforce regardless of
the license chosen.

RATIONALE

MIT is the most permissive common license and the one that introduces the least
friction for contributors, downstream users, and the project's own operation.
The goal of the project is for transparent quantitative finance methodology to
spread and become normal rather than exceptional. Ideas spread fastest when they
are unencumbered. The protection that more restrictive licenses offer is real in
principle but contingent on enforcement capacity the project does not have. MIT
makes the values of the project legible through openness rather than through
legal mechanism.

CONSEQUENCES

Positive: contributor onboarding is unimpeded by license concerns. The project's
work can be used, adapted, and built upon with minimal friction. The license is
universally understood and compatible with essentially all other open source
licenses.

Negative: there is no legal mechanism preventing a well-resourced actor from
taking the project's research, incorporating it into a proprietary product, and
redistributing it without reciprocity. Attribution is required but reciprocity
is not.

Neutral: the copyright line currently names a single individual. This is normal
for a project at this stage but does not reflect the collective nature of open
source contribution. A transition to a foundation or similar structure holding
copyright on behalf of the project would be an appropriate next step at later
stages.
